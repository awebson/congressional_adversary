We are researchers from Brown University trying to help computers understand political euphemism! If you pass this test, we will approve your qualification usually within 4 hours and no later than 12 hours.

In order to convey an opinion or push an agenda, politicians from different parties often use very different words to refer to the same underlying policies or ideas, even though the sentiment, opinion, or connotation of those phrases might vary dramatically.

You will be shown a series of phrase pairs. Please indicate on a 5-point scale how much the phrases overlap in the underlying event or policy they refer to. You should ignore differences that stem from opinion or stance, i.e. the way the speaker feels about the topic. Examples of each point on the scale are below.

The examples are drawn from transcripts of congressional floor speeches from 1981 to 2011. They were automatically converted to digital form from paper transcripts. Consequently, phrases often are transcribed without spaces or punctuation, e.g. “cap-and-trade” may appear as “capandtrade”. We apologize for these errors.

That is, if you are reasonably familiar with US politics, you should be able to make many judgments using your existing knowledge alone. However, if you are unsure what a word or phrase means, please use the provided Google search links. We don’t expect you to know every phrase.
